Section 3.2 Convergence Tests for Sequences

As a real-valued function, we understand what means by a sequence of real numbers is increasing (or decreasing). A sequence is monotone if it is either increasing or decreasing.

The following statement is intuitively clear. Its validity relies on the completeness of real numbers. It allows ones to argue the convergence of a sequence without knowing its limit.

Theorem 3.2.1. Monotonic Convergence Theorem.

Every increasing (decreasing) sequence that is bounded above (below) converges.

As an application of the monotonic convergence theorem, we show that:

Example 3.2.2.

\(\displaystyle \lim_{n\to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n\) exists.

By Theorem 3.2.1, it suffices to argue that \(a_n:=(1+1/n)^n\) form a bounded increasing sequence.

It follows from the Binomial Theorem that

\begin{equation*} a_n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k}. \end{equation*}

Since for all \(k \ge 1\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \ds \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} = \frac{n(n-1)\cdots (n-k+1)}{k!}\frac{1}{n^k} \lt \frac{1}{k!} \le \frac{1}{2^{k-1}}, \end{equation*}

and so \(\ds a_n \le 1 + \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{2^{k-1}} = 1 + 2 = 3.\) It remains to show that \((a_n)\) is increasing.

For each \(0 \le k \le n\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \binom{n+1}{k}\frac{1}{(n+1)^k} = \frac{1}{k!}\prod_{i=0}^k \left(1 - \frac{i}{n+1}\right) \ge \frac{1}{k!}\prod_{i=0}^k \left(1 - \frac{i}{n}\right) = \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k}. \end{equation*}

So,

\begin{equation*} a_n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{n^k} \le \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n+1}{k}\frac{1}{(n+1)^k} \lt \sum_{k=0}^{n+1} \binom{n+1}{k}\frac{1}{(n+1)^k} = a_{n+1}. \end{equation*}

This completes the proof.

The limit of \((1+1/n)^n\) can be taken as the definition of \(e\text{,}\) the base of the natural logarithm.

The following proposition is another application of the monotonic convergence theorem.

Proposition 3.2.3.

The sequence \((c^n)\) converges to \(0\) if \(0 \lt c \lt 1\) or unbounded if \(c > 1\text{.}\)

Proof.

Suppose \(0 \lt c \lt 1\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} c \gt c^2 \gt c^3 \gt \cdots \end{equation*}

is a decreasing sequence. Moreover, the product of two positive numbers is positive, the sequence \((c^n)\) is convergent by the monotonic convergence theorem 3.2.1 and let \(L\) be its limit. On the one hand, since the sequence

\begin{equation*} c^2 , c^3 , c^4, \ldots \end{equation*}

is just \((c^n)\) with its first term dropped, it also converges to \(L\text{.}\) On the other hand, this sequence is \(c(c^n) =(c^{n+1})\) so it converges to \(cL\text{.}\) Thus, by the uniqueness of limits, \(cL = c\) and so \(L\) must be \(0\) since \(c \neq 0\text{.}\)

If \(c \gt 1\text{,}\) say \(c = 1+a\) for some \(a \gt 0\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} c^n = (1+a)^n = 1+ na + \text{positive terms} \gt 1+na \to \infty \end{equation*}

as \(n \to \infty\text{.}\)

Proposition 3.2.4. Ratio Test for sequence convergence.

Let \((x_n)\) be a sequence of non-zero real numbers and the limit \(\lim_n |x_{n+1}|/|x_n|\) exists. Then

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} (x_n)\ \text{is null} & \text{if}\ L \lt 1 \\ (x_n)\ \text{is unbounded} & \text{if}\ L \gt 1. \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Let \((x_n)\) and \((y_n)\) be two sequences of positive real numbers. We write \((x_n) \ll (y_n)\) if the sequence of ratios \((x_n/y_n)\) is null. Intuitively, \((x_n) \ll (y_n)\) means \(y_n\) is way bigger than \(x_n\) eventually.

Proposition 3.2.5.

For any \(a \ge 0\) and \(b \gt 1\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} n^a \ll b^n \ll n! \ll n^n \end{equation*}

Proof.

Since \(b \gt 1\text{,}\) \(\ds \frac{(n+1)^a}{b^{n+1}}\frac{b^n}{n^a} = \left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)^a \frac{1}{b} \to \frac{1}{b} \lt 1\text{,}\) so \(\ds \frac{n^a}{b^n} \to 0\) by the ratio test 3.2.4.

Also, \(\ds \frac{b^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}\frac{n!}{b^n} = \frac{b}{n+1} \to 0\text{,}\) thus \(\ds \frac{b^n}{n!} \to 0\) by the ratio test.

Since

\begin{equation*} 0 \le \frac{n!}{n^n} = \frac{1}{n}\frac{2}{n} \cdots \frac{n}{n} \lt \frac{1}{n}, \end{equation*}

So \(n!/n^n \to 0\) by the Squeeze Lemma 3.1.14.

Example 3.2.6.

\(n^{1/n} \to 1\text{.}\) Since \(1 \le n^{1/n}\text{,}\) it suffices to show that for any \(\varepsilon \gt 0, n^{1/n} \lt 1+\varepsilon\) eventually. But this follows immediate from Proposition 3.2.5 as \(n \ll (1+\varepsilon)^n\text{.}\)

Example 3.2.7.

Let us compute the limit of the sequence given by \(a_n = (e^{2n} + n)^{1/n}\) in another way. Note that

\begin{equation*} e^{2} \lt a_n=e^2\left(1 +\frac{n}{e^{2n}}\right)^{1/n} \lt e^2(2^{1/n}) \end{equation*}

since \(n \lt 2^n \lt e^{2n}\text{.}\) By Exercise 3.7.2 \(2^{1/n} \to 1\) and so \(a_n \to e^2\) by the Squeeze Lemma.
